Travelling to Australia: Insights

Using data from the ABS, this project visualises where overseas visitors travel, which countries they come from, and their main reasons for visiting through a range of Vega-Lite maps and charts

Total Arrivals in each State

Since 1991, tourism arrivals have grown dramatically across all Australian states and territories. Every region now receives far more visitors than three decades ago. Victoria shows the biggest jump with growth over 540%, followed by Western Australia (445%) and Tasmania (377%). Even the slowest growth, in the Northern Territory (225%), means arrivals more than tripled. Nationally, total arrivals have climbed from about 884,000 in 1991 to over 21 million in 2025, roughly a fourfold increase. This strong, consistent upward trend highlights how tourism has become a major part of Australia’s economy and identity.

Who visit Australia the most?

The chart shows the top 15 countries by total arrivals to Australia. New Zealand leads by a large margin, contributing nearly 18.7% of all international visitors, almost one in five arrivals. The United Kingdom follows at 12.2%, reflecting strong historical and cultural ties. China (8.7%) and Japan (8.2%) are major Asian sources of visitors, while the United States (7.2%) rounds out the top five. Overall, the data highlights that Australia’s tourism is dominated by regional neighbours and English-speaking countries, which shape Australia’s visitor base.

Has tourism recover after Covid?

The chart shows how total visitor arrivals in Australia changed from 2015 to 2025. Visitor numbers were stable before 2020, averaging around 1.5 to 2 million per month. However, after COVID-19 restrictions began in March 2020, arrivals collapsed almost to zero — the sharpest drop in modern tourism history. It took until late 2022 for visitor numbers to begin a steady recovery, and by 2024–2025 arrivals had largely returned to pre-pandemic levels. This pattern clearly highlights the severe but temporary impact of the pandemic on Australia’s tourism industry.

Main reasons of short-term visitors

The chart shows how visitor arrivals by purpose changed from 2015 onwards. Before 2020, holidays and visiting friends or relatives were the most common reasons for travel. When COVID-19 restrictions began, all categories dropped sharply, especially holiday travel, which nearly disappeared for almost two years. Arrivals for education and employment also fell but started recovering earlier as borders reopened for students and workers. By 2023–2025, most travel purposes had rebounded, though leisure travel remained below pre-pandemic highs. This pattern highlights how different types of travel were affected and recovered at different speeds after the pandemic.

When do most people visit Australia?

This chart compares monthly visitor patterns from 2015 to 2024, revealing clear seasonal trends in Australia’s tourism. Visitor numbers peak sharply in the summer months of December, January, and February, reflecting holiday travel and warm weather attractions. Mid-year months such as May to September typically record fewer arrivals, forming the tourism off-season. Despite year-to-year fluctuations, this recurring pattern shows that Australia’s tourism demand is strongly shaped by seasonal preferences, with the top three months being December, January, and February.

How long do short-term visitors stay in Australia?

This chart shows the share of visitors by length of stay in Australia from 2015 to 2025. Before 2020, most visitors stayed for less than one month, with short-term trips under two weeks making up the largest share. During the COVID-19 period (2020–2021), the pattern shifted dramatically — long stays of over six months suddenly became dominant, as border closures limited short visits and only long-term visa holders or residents could remain in the country. After 2022, short-term travel gradually recovered, and by 2024–2025 the visitor mix had largely returned to its pre-pandemic structure. Overall, the data highlights how the pandemic temporarily reversed Australia’s usual pattern of short holiday stays.
